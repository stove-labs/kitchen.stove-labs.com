Signatures

Public key cryptography and its digital signatures belong to the building blocks of a blockchain. They are always used when a piece of information needs to be written to the blockchain ledger. So called transactions carry the instructions to do so, but a digital signature is required to proof that it comes from the owner or authorized entity. In other words, the transaction initiator needs to authorize the action. The protocol takes care of validating or checking of the correctness of the signature.

Consider that Alice has created two keys, a private and a public key. Those are not the same keys, but they are mathematically connected1. She keeps her private key, as the name suggests, private at all times. She uses it to digitally sign a message (or transaction). Anyone with Alice' pubilc key is able to verify that it was signed with the corresponding private key.

In practice, the public key is linked to an address. The address represents an account that holds a balance. Whenever a user instructs to change its balance by performing a transfer transaction to another user, the protocol that is executed by a node, performs a check on the signature. In other words, it makes sure that the owner of the account authorizes the change.
